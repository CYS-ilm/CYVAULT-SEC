Social Engineering Attacks Explained

By CYVault Research Desk · Reviewed by the Security Awareness Team

Introduction

Social engineering is the art of manipulating people into revealing confidential information or performing actions that benefit an attacker. Instead of exploiting software vulnerabilities, these attacks exploit human trust, curiosity, fear, or urgency. Even organisations with strong technical defences can fall victim if employees are not adequately prepared.

Common Types of Social Engineering

  • Phishing — Fraudulent emails designed to steal credentials or install malware.
  • Spear Phishing — Targeted attacks crafted specifically for an individual or organisation.
  • Whaling — Phishing attacks aimed at executives and senior decision-makers.
  • Vishing — Voice calls impersonating trusted organisations, colleagues, or IT support.
  • Smishing — SMS messages containing malicious links or fraudulent requests.
  • Pretexting — Attackers invent a believable story to obtain sensitive information or access.
  • Baiting — Offering something attractive, such as a free USB drive or download, to trick victims.
  • Tailgating — Following authorised personnel into restricted physical areas without credentials.

Warning Signs

  • Unexpected requests for passwords, credentials, or sensitive data.
  • Messages creating urgency, fear, or pressure to act immediately.
  • Suspicious links, unexpected attachments, or unusual file formats.
  • Requests to bypass normal security procedures or approval workflows.
  • Poor grammar, unusual email addresses, or unfamiliar or spoofed senders.

How to Protect Your Organisation

  • Conduct regular security awareness training for all employees.
  • Verify unusual requests through trusted, independent communication channels.
  • Enable Multi-Factor Authentication (MFA) on all critical accounts and systems.
  • Use email filtering, anti-phishing gateways, and DNS-level protection.
  • Encourage employees to report suspicious activity without fear of blame.
  • Run periodic phishing simulations to measure and improve employee awareness.
